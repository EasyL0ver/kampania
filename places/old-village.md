# %OLD_VILLAGE%

**Type:** Lemko village, Bieszczady region

## Before 1947

A small Lemko settlement in the San River valley. Typical of the region — wooden *chyża* homesteads, a *cerkiew* (wooden church) with onion dome, surrounding orchards and fields. Greek Catholic community. The village would have had a few dozen families at most, tightly knit, self-sufficient.

## The Rebellion & Massacre (1947)

During Operation Vistula, when soldiers arrived to deport the inhabitants of %OLD_VILLAGE%, the Lemko population resisted. The situation escalated into open rebellion against the deportation forces.

The soldiers responded with lethal force. The entire rebelling population was shot.

The massacre was **never reported**. The participating soldiers made a pact of silence — no one would ever speak of what happened at %OLD_VILLAGE%. The event was swept under the rug completely.

## After 1947

Officially, %OLD_VILLAGE% was simply "evacuated" like every other village. No records of resistance, no records of deaths. **The village was burned.** Soldiers set fire to the wooden buildings to erase traces — standard procedure during Akcja Wisła. The wooden *chyża* homesteads went up. The orchards charred. The attempt was thorough.

But it didn't work completely. **The stone survived.** The cerkiew — partially stone-built — burned but didn't collapse entirely. Stone foundations, chimneys, and cellar walls withstood the fire. The well is stone-lined. You can burn a village but you can't burn stone, and you can't burn what's underground.

By the 1960s, the ruins of %OLD_VILLAGE% sit in the flood zone of the planned Solina reservoir. When the water rises, all physical evidence will vanish. What fire couldn't finish, water will.

## Buildings & Locations (1967)

- **Wooden Greek Catholic church with onion dome** — most identifiable structure
- **Current state:** Roof partially collapsed on west side; interior exposed; wood rotting; some icons remain faded on interior walls
- **Access:** Door hangs loose; interior wet from rain and groundwater seepage
- **Interior remnants:** Abandoned altar, broken candle stands, debris on floor
- **Evidence:** Fresh candles appear periodically — signs that someone visits to perform rituals (%HAG%)
- **Practical note:** Potentially unstable; ceiling could collapse in sections

### Cemetery
- **Adjacent to church** — marked by Cyrillic stone crosses and headstones
- **Current state:** Headstones tilted or toppled; inscriptions weathered; graves subsiding as wood coffins rot
- **Ground:** Soggy, overgrown with vegetation; difficult to walk through
- **Numbers:** Fewer graves than would be expected for a village population
- **Practical concern:** The discrepancy — where are the rest of the dead? — is immediately visible

### Homestead Ruins (Multiple)
- **Stone foundations and chimneys** scattered across the area — all that remains of wooden *chyża* homes
- **Current state:** Foundations 1–2 feet above ground level; chimneys partially intact; interiors flooded or filled with debris

- **Remnants visible:** Pottery shards, rusted tools, fragments of daily life scattered on surface
- **Fruit trees:** Overgrown orchards still bearing apples and plums — eerie witness to human abandonment
- **Practical hazard:** Hidden cellar holes, unstable stones, flooding in cellars
