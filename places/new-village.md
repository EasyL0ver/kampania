# %NEW_VILLAGE%

**Type:** Polish settler village, Bieszczady region

## Founded

1950, three years after Operation Vistula (1947). Built adjacent to the ruins of %OLD_VILLAGE%.

## Origin

Part of the state-organized resettlement wave to repopulate the emptied Bieszczady. Polish colonists — likely a mix of:
- Repatriates from Poland's lost eastern territories (Kresy)
- Farmers from central/southern Poland seeking land
- People escaping their pasts — ex-convicts, drifters, adventurers
- State-directed workers (forestry, PGR state farms)

The authorities presented Bieszczady as a "promised land." The reality: ruined buildings, neglected soil, no infrastructure, isolation, and fear of remaining UPA bands.

## Relationship to %OLD_VILLAGE%

%NEW_VILLAGE% sits right next to the old Lemko settlement. The colonists would have seen the ruins daily — the decaying *cerkiew*, the overgrown cemetery with Cyrillic inscriptions, the crumbling *chyża* homesteads, fruit trees bearing for no one.

What they would **not** know: that %OLD_VILLAGE% wasn't simply evacuated. That the people who lived there didn't leave — they were killed. That the bones are still there.

## Life in %NEW_VILLAGE%

- Small, struggling community. Harsh conditions, primitive infrastructure.
- No shared history among settlers — strangers from different regions thrown together.
- Living in the shadow of a dead village they know nothing about.
- By the 1960s: facing displacement themselves — the Solina Dam project will flood their village too.

## Story Significance

The colonists of %NEW_VILLAGE% are the second layer of this tragedy. Brought in to fill the void left by ethnic cleansing, they now face their own expulsion by the state. And they've been living next to an unmarked mass grave without knowing it.
