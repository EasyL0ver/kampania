# Michał Pytlak Saves the Village — The Engineering Ending

**Type:** Story ending — one of the possible resolutions

## What It Is

Michał Pytlak has a plan to save the village from the flood. Not sandbags. Not prayers. **Explosives.** He wants to blow a channel through a rock formation to redirect the floodwater away from %NEW_VILLAGE% — a controlled detonation that sends the water somewhere it can't hurt anyone.

It's insane. It's dangerous. It might work.

## The Explosives

The UPA bunker in the forest contains old partisan munitions — unstable, decades-old ordnance from the 1940s. Michał Pytlak knows about the bunker (everyone who works the land around the forest does, even if nobody talks about it). He knows there are explosives in there.

Getting them out is the problem:
- The bunker is partially collapsed, flooded in sections, structurally unstable.
- The ordnance is old — possibly degraded, possibly still live. Handling it wrong kills you.
- Someone has to go in, find what's usable, and carry it out without dying.
- **The players can help with this** — or refuse. It's their risk too.

## The Plan

Michał Pytlak knows the terrain. He's worked this land for years — every slope, every drainage path, every place the water naturally wants to go. There's a point where a rock formation acts as a natural dam, directing floodwater toward the village. Blow it, and the water goes around instead of through.

It's not a guess. He's thought about it. He's walked the ground. He's right about the geography. The question is whether anyone trusts a 57-year-old farm overseer with stolen partisan explosives enough to let him try.

## What the Players Must Do

1. **Believe him** — Michał Pytlak isn't a military engineer. He's a farmer who reads terrain. The players need to look at his plan and decide it's not suicide.
2. **Get the explosives** — Enter the UPA bunker, find usable ordnance, bring it out alive. This costs time and risk.
3. **Support the detonation** — Help place the charges, clear the area, manage the blast. Or stand back and pray.

## The Ambiguity

A 57-year-old man who hasn't slept in days, running on inhuman fortitude the well gave him, using explosives from a bunker full of ghosts, executing a plan that shouldn't work but does.

Did he save the village because he's that good at reading land? Or did something give him what he needed — the endurance to plan, the clarity to see the solution, the strength to carry it out?

**The story never answers this.** The blast works. The water turns. The village stands. Michał Pytlak sits down afterward and looks like a man who aged ten years in five days.

## What It Means

The ritual ending (Stefania Kopacz) addresses the dead — the past, the guilt, the spiritual wound.
The engineering ending (Michał Pytlak) addresses the living — the flood, the homes, the future.

Both require the players to recognise which person matters and support them. Stefania Kopacz needs a chain of trust (players → ks. Władysław Pająk → Barbara Kopacz → Stefania Kopacz). Michał Pytlak needs belief and hands — players choosing explosives over clipboards.

**The best outcome is both.** The dead are acknowledged. The living are saved. But the players probably can't do everything — time is the resource, and every hour on one ending is an hour not on the other.

## Connection Points

- **The UPA bunker** — Finally has a narrative purpose beyond atmosphere. The old war feeds the new survival.
- **Zbigniew Gajda** — If the village survives, his evacuation plan was unnecessary. His people are alive. But the village stays, the well stays, and the secrets stay buried under dry land.
- **Marek Gajda** — No flood means no reimbursement, no escape to the city. Michał Pytlak saving the village traps him here.
- **Stefania Kopacz** — If the village survives, the woman ready to die doesn't have to.
- **Paraskewia Chyłak** — Her cabin in the forest may still be destroyed. The water has to go somewhere.
- **The cover-up** — The man who saved the village got a worker killed through negligence and buried the truth. Does that still matter?
