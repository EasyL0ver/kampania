# %FOREMAN% Saves the Village — The Engineering Ending

**Type:** Story ending — one of the possible resolutions

## What It Is

%FOREMAN% has a plan to save the village from the flood. Not sandbags. Not prayers. **Explosives.** He wants to blow a channel through a rock formation to redirect the floodwater away from %NEW_VILLAGE% — a controlled detonation that sends the water somewhere it can't hurt anyone.

It's insane. It's dangerous. It might work.

## The Explosives

The UPA bunker in the forest contains old partisan munitions — unstable, decades-old ordnance from the 1940s. %FOREMAN% knows about the bunker (everyone who works the land around the forest does, even if nobody talks about it). He knows there are explosives in there.

Getting them out is the problem:
- The bunker is partially collapsed, flooded in sections, structurally unstable.
- The ordnance is old — possibly degraded, possibly still live. Handling it wrong kills you.
- Someone has to go in, find what's usable, and carry it out without dying.
- **The players can help with this** — or refuse. It's their risk too.

## The Plan

%FOREMAN% knows the terrain. He's worked this land for years — every slope, every drainage path, every place the water naturally wants to go. There's a point where a rock formation acts as a natural dam, directing floodwater toward the village. Blow it, and the water goes around instead of through.

It's not a guess. He's thought about it. He's walked the ground. He's right about the geography. The question is whether anyone trusts a 57-year-old farm overseer with stolen partisan explosives enough to let him try.

## What the Players Must Do

1. **Believe him** — %FOREMAN% isn't a military engineer. He's a farmer who reads terrain. The players need to look at his plan and decide it's not suicide.
2. **Get the explosives** — Enter the UPA bunker, find usable ordnance, bring it out alive. This costs time and risk.
3. **Support the detonation** — Help place the charges, clear the area, manage the blast. Or stand back and pray.

## The Ambiguity

A 57-year-old man who hasn't slept in days, running on inhuman fortitude the well gave him, using explosives from a bunker full of ghosts, executing a plan that shouldn't work but does.

Did he save the village because he's that good at reading land? Or did something give him what he needed — the endurance to plan, the clarity to see the solution, the strength to carry it out?

**The story never answers this.** The blast works. The water turns. The village stands. %FOREMAN% sits down afterward and looks like a man who aged ten years in five days.

## What It Means

The ritual ending (%BABCIA%) addresses the dead — the past, the guilt, the spiritual wound.
The engineering ending (%FOREMAN%) addresses the living — the flood, the homes, the future.

Both require the players to recognise which person matters and support them. %BABCIA% needs a chain of trust (players → %PRIEST% → %BARBARA% → %BABCIA%). %FOREMAN% needs belief and hands — players choosing explosives over clipboards.

**The best outcome is both.** The dead are acknowledged. The living are saved. But the players probably can't do everything — time is the resource, and every hour on one ending is an hour not on the other.

## Connection Points

- **The UPA bunker** — Finally has a narrative purpose beyond atmosphere. The old war feeds the new survival.
- **%WOJEWODA%** — If the village survives, his evacuation plan was unnecessary. His people are alive. But the village stays, the well stays, and the secrets stay buried under dry land.
- **%JUNIOR%** — No flood means no reimbursement, no escape to the city. %FOREMAN% saving the village traps him here.
- **%BABCIA%** — If the village survives, the woman ready to die doesn't have to.
- **%HAG%** — Her cabin in the forest may still be destroyed. The water has to go somewhere.
- **The embezzlement** — The man who saved the village is the man who's been stealing from it. Does that still matter?
