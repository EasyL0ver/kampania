# The Bimber Still — Red Herring

**Type:** Story fact / red herring / investigation trap

## What It Is

Tadek Gajda and his drinking crew (Szymek Kępa, Romek Głowacz, Franek Mucha) run an illegal moonshine (bimber) operation deep in the forest between %NEW_VILLAGE% and %OLD_VILLAGE%. A crude copper still hidden under camouflage netting near a stream, surrounded by empty sugar sacks, barrels, and firewood.

## Location

A small clearing off one of the forest paths, roughly halfway between the two villages. Deliberately placed away from both — far enough from %NEW_VILLAGE% that the smoke isn't visible, close enough to the stream for water supply. The path to it is subtle but worn — someone who looks carefully at foot traffic in the forest will find it.

## Why It Matters (For Investigation)

When the committee starts asking questions or exploring the forest, they're likely to stumble on this operation — or notice signs of it (foot traffic, smoke smell, sugar deliveries, Tadek Gajda disappearing into the woods). It looks **deeply suspicious**:

- Secret activity in the forest? Must be connected to the cover-up.
- Tadek Gajda sneaking around? He's hiding something — but what?
- A hidden site between the two villages? Maybe this is where evidence was buried.
- The drinking crew gets nervous and hostile when questioned? They're clearly protecting a secret.

**The secret is just bimber.** Illegal, yes. Connected to the murders, no.

## Why It Works as a Red Herring

1. **It wastes player time.** Investigating the still, confronting the crew, following the forest trails — all of it costs actions in a game with only three per day.
2. **It creates real tension for the wrong reason.** The drinking crew genuinely *is* hiding something and genuinely *will* react with hostility, threats, or evasion when pressed. Tadek Gajda might panic — not because of the lynch, but because he thinks the state committee is here to bust his still. His fear is real; his reason is wrong.
3. **It muddies Tadek Gajda's behavior.** He's the weakest link in the lynch cover-up AND he's paranoid about the bimber. Players can't easily tell which secret is making him sweat. When he gets evasive or aggressive, is it guilt over murder or fear of a moonshine charge?
4. **It pulls players toward the forest for the wrong reason.** They may find the still before they find %OLD_VILLAGE%, the well, or Paraskewia Chyłak's cabin — and decide the forest is "solved."

## How Players Might Find It

- Following Tadek Gajda when he disappears into the forest
- Smelling wood smoke or fermentation in the woods
- Noticing Szymek Kępa or Franek Mucha hauling sugar sacks from the store
- Halina Zając at the store complaining about bulk sugar purchases
- Stumbling on the worn path during general forest exploration

## Crew Reactions If Discovered

- **Tadek Gajda** — Panics. Assumes the committee will report them. May try to bribe, threaten, or beg. His panic looks identical to guilty-conscience behavior, which makes him look even more suspicious for the wrong reasons.
- **Szymek Kępa** — Belligerent. "State people sticking their noses where they don't belong." Might try to intimidate.
- **Romek Głowacz** — Quiet, calculating. Watches the players. Might try to gauge what they actually know vs. what they're fishing for.
- **Franek Mucha** — Aggressive. The most likely to escalate to physical confrontation. Sees the players as a direct threat.

## Connections to Larger Story

- **Zbigniew Gajda knows about the still** — and tolerates it. It keeps Tadek Gajda and the crew occupied, dependent, and manageable. If the players report it to him, he'll wave it off or promise to "handle it" — another sign of his control.
- **Helena Gajda supplies the sugar** through her store — at a markup. She profits from her brother's addiction. Another thread that looks conspiratorial but is just commerce.
- **The still site is NOT near the well or Paraskewia Chyłak's cabin** — but it's in the same forest. Players who "clear" the forest after finding the still may not look deeper. Players who keep exploring despite the still may find the real secrets.

## For the GM

The bimber operation should feel like a breakthrough when discovered — a hidden thing in the forest, nervous men, secrecy. Let the players ride that high for a beat before the realization settles in: this is just moonshine. The disappointment is the point. It teaches them that not every secret in %NEW_VILLAGE% is connected to murder — and it burns an action they can't get back.

If players handle the discovery with discretion (not reporting it, using it as leverage), it can become a **tool**: "We know about your still. Now tell us what else you know." Tadek Gajda under that pressure — owing the players a favor, scared they'll talk — is more vulnerable to cracking about the real secret.
