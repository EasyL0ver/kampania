# Irena Gajda and Marek Gajda's Secret Investigation — Red Herring

**Type:** Story fact / red herring / parallel investigation

## What It Is

Irena Gajda has been quietly investigating the events of 1954 for years. Not systematically — she has no training, no resources, no allies. Just a woman who can't stop noticing. But in the last year or two, she gained something she never had before: **Marek Gajda**.

He's 21 now. Old enough to be trusted. Old enough to ask questions. And old enough to have noticed on his own that something is deeply wrong with this family.

## How It Started

Irena Gajda never sat Marek Gajda down and told him. It happened gradually — a comment here, a question there. Marek Gajda grew up watching his father's locked silences, his uncle's drinking, his aunt Helena Gajda's controlled performances. He started asking his mother: *"Why does uncle Tadek Gajda drink so much?"* and *"What happened to the family that used to live in Janina Gajda's house?"*

Irena Gajda didn't answer directly. But she stopped deflecting. She'd say things like *"Nobody talks about that"* or *"Ask your father and watch his face."* That was enough. Marek Gajda started watching. Then noticing. Then asking more.

Now they have an unspoken pact. She watches the house — Zbigniew Gajda's papers, his meetings with por. Witold Skowron, the locked silences between the siblings. Marek Gajda works the outside — listening to Tadek Gajda when he's drunk, poking around the village, visiting %OLD_VILLAGE% when nobody's watching.

## What They've Found

Not much. Enough to be dangerous, not enough to understand:

- **Irena Gajda's collection:** A stained shirt from 1954 she never threw away. A button she found in the yard that didn't match any of Zbigniew Gajda's clothes. Newspaper clippings about missing persons that never matched anyone. Notes in her handwriting — dates, observations, fragments of overheard conversations. Hidden in a flour tin in the pantry.
- **Marek Gajda's fragments from Tadek Gajda:** Drunken mumbles. *"She didn't deserve it."* A name — Hania Barnaś — spoken once, never repeated. Tadek Gajda grabbing Marek Gajda's arm one night and saying *"Don't ever be like us"* before passing out. Nothing concrete. Everything damning in tone.
- **Fresh tracks at %OLD_VILLAGE%:** Marek Gajda has been visiting the ruins. He's seen the well, the cemetery, the cerkiew. He doesn't know what he's looking for. He's felt the wrongness of the place without understanding it.
- **A partial timeline:** Between them, they've roughly established that something happened in 1954, that it involved Edward Barnaś's family, and that all four siblings were somehow connected. They don't know what. They don't have proof. Just a shape in the dark.

## Why It's a Red Herring

When the committee arrives and starts investigating, they'll encounter signs of Irena Gajda and Marek Gajda's parallel investigation:

- **Fresh footprints at %OLD_VILLAGE%** that aren't Paraskewia Chyłak's. Someone has been there recently.
- **Marek Gajda asking Ryszard Dudka strange questions** — the players hear about this second-hand.
- **Irena Gajda acting suspiciously** — hovering near conversations, watching the committee, clearly wanting to approach but pulling back.
- **Marek Gajda disappearing into the forest** — same pattern as the bimber crew, same pattern as a conspirator.
- **The hidden collection** — if players search Zbigniew Gajda's house, they might find Irena Gajda's flour tin before they find anything of Zbigniew Gajda's. A stained shirt and notes that look like a cover-up dossier.

Players will spend time trying to figure out who this second investigator is. Are they being followed? Is someone destroying evidence ahead of them? Is Marek Gajda working for his father? Is Irena Gajda a co-conspirator maintaining a blackmail file?

**The truth:** A mother and son who've been doing exactly what the players are doing — just worse, longer, and with no authority behind them.

## The Danger

Irena Gajda and Marek Gajda's investigation is the most dangerous thing in the village — not for the players, but for *them*:

- **If Zbigniew Gajda finds out**, his wife and son have been building a case against him. He'll see betrayal, not justice. What he does next depends on how cornered he feels.
- **If Helena Gajda finds out**, she'll move to neutralize it. She's done this before — she knows how to handle people who become problems.
- **If Stanisław Rezeń finds out**, Marek Gajda has been visiting %OLD_VILLAGE% and asking about 1954. The man who tried to strangle a toddler to eliminate witnesses won't hesitate.
- **If por. Witold Skowron finds out**, state security has a new problem — civilians digging into events the state wants buried.

The players' investigation and Irena Gajda/Marek Gajda's investigation are on a collision course. When they meet, it can go several ways:

- **Alliance:** The players gain two inside sources with 13 years of observation. Irena Gajda's fragments + the committee's authority = real progress. But it puts all of them at greater risk.
- **Catastrophe:** The players inadvertently expose Irena Gajda's investigation to Zbigniew Gajda by asking the wrong question, showing the stained shirt, or mentioning Marek Gajda's visits to %OLD_VILLAGE%. Now the family knows there's a traitor inside the house.
- **Mistrust:** Irena Gajda decides the committee can't be trusted (they're too close to Zbigniew Gajda, too friendly with por. Witold Skowron) and actively works against them — hiding evidence, warning Marek Gajda to stop, retreating into silence.

## Marek Gajda's Role

Marek Gajda is not just helping his mother — he's becoming his own person through this investigation. The spoiled sołtys's kid who drank with Tadek Gajda and slept with Barbara Kopacz is now carrying a secret that could destroy his entire family. He's growing up fast.

His closeness to Tadek Gajda is now double-edged: he genuinely likes his uncle, AND he's mining him for information. Every drinking session is both real bonding and covert interrogation. Marek Gajda hates this — using the uncle who loves him as a source.

His visits to %OLD_VILLAGE% are reckless. He doesn't know what's in the well. He doesn't know about Paraskewia Chyłak. He's stumbling around a place full of secrets he's not ready for, leaving tracks that others can follow.

## For the GM

This subplot serves multiple purposes:

- **Red herring:** Players waste time identifying the "second investigator" and sorting friend from foe.
- **Time pressure:** Marek Gajda's reckless visits to %OLD_VILLAGE% risk alerting Zbigniew Gajda, Stanisław Rezeń, or Helena Gajda. If the players don't find Irena Gajda and Marek Gajda quickly, the family might find them first.
- **Emotional payoff:** When the players finally connect with Irena Gajda, it's one of the most powerful moments in the story — two investigations meeting, a woman who's waited 13 years for someone to believe her.
- **Tragedy potential:** If the players fumble the connection — expose Irena Gajda to Zbigniew Gajda, or arrive too late — this subplot becomes the story's deepest wound. The one person trying to do the right thing, destroyed by the people who came to help.
