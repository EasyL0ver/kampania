# Tadeusz Mazur's Death — The Foreman's Cover-Up

**Type:** Story fact / red herring / investigation thread

## What Happened

About two years ago (~1965), Tadeusz Mazur died in a grain silo on the PGR farm. A blockage in the concrete silo — grain caked and wouldn't flow. Someone had to go in and break it loose. Tadeusz volunteered, or Michał Pytlak asked him because he was the strongest. Neither of them knew grain could behave like quicksand.

Tadeusz went in. The crust gave way. He dropped through the grain and was buried in seconds — engulfed to the chest, then gone. Michał Pytlak went in after him. The other workers had to drag the Foreman out before they lost both men. By the time they dug Tadeusz free with their hands, he had suffocated.

No safety harness. No ventilation check. No entry procedure. Not because Michał Pytlak cut corners — because nobody on a remote Bieszczady PGR in 1967 had ever been taught these things existed. He didn't know what he didn't know. But a state inspector would write *"criminal negligence leading to death"* — no harness, no procedure, no training, supervisor sent worker into a confined space. On paper, it's clear-cut. The fact that the Foreman tried to save him with his bare hands doesn't appear in the report.

If the death were reported, there would be a state inspection. The farm would be investigated — unsafe conditions, no safety equipment, criminal negligence, Michał Pytlak's responsibility. The farm could be shut down or restructured. And from Zbigniew Gajda's perspective: state inspectors crawling over his village, asking questions, looking at records, talking to people. He cannot have that. Not ever.

So the two men agreed: **the death was never reported.**

## How It Works Now

- **Tadeusz Mazur is still "alive" on state records.** The PGR ledger lists him as a current worker. His wages are drawn every month.
- **Wanda Mazur receives his wages.** She was told it's a **widow's pension** — proper state benefit for a bereaved wife. She believes this completely. She's grateful to the state for taking care of her.
- **The village doesn't know** the death was never officially reported. Everyone saw the funeral, everyone knows Tadeusz is dead. They assume it was handled properly — death certificate filed, pension granted, all done the normal way.
- **Only two men know the truth:** Zbigniew Gajda and Michał Pytlak. The sołtys made the call. The foreman signs the ledger.
- **Zofia Pytlak knows.** Michał told her the night it happened — he couldn't carry it alone. She's been pushing him to report it for two years. She wants the truth out, wants her husband free of the lie. She's the conscience he can't listen to. See [Zofia's character file](../characters/zofia.md).

## Why Michał Pytlak Carries This

He's not a corrupt man. He's a good man who sent someone into a grain silo without knowing it could kill him, then tried to pull the man out with his bare hands and failed. He signed a cover-up on his boss's orders, and now pays the dead man's wife with phantom wages every month. He doesn't profit from it — every złoty of Tadeusz's wages goes to Wanda. But he signs his name next to a dead man's every month and he knows what it means.

The guilt is real. The kindness is real. The lie is real. All three coexist.

## What Players Might Notice

- **The ledger has a name nobody recognises.** Tadeusz Mazur is listed as a current PGR worker. But nobody at the farm answers to that name. The workers don't mention him.
- **Wanda Mazur at the cemetery** openly says her husband is dead. She has no reason to hide it — she thinks everything was handled properly. If players have the ledger, the name matches a "current" worker. He's been dead for two years.
- **Michał Pytlak gets uncomfortable** when asked about Tadeusz specifically. He makes excuses — "hasn't been in lately," "seasonal, comes and goes." He's a bad liar under direct questioning.

## Why It Looks Suspicious

A government committee cross-referencing the worker registry against the census finds a living worker whose widow says he's been dead for two years. The investigation reflex kicks in:

- Why is a dead man still on the payroll? Who's collecting his wages?
- Why wasn't his death reported? What are they hiding?
- Is Zbigniew Gajda involved? Is this how the village operates — burying inconvenient truths?
- Why does Michał Pytlak panic when they ask about the books?

**The answer:** a man suffocated in a grain silo because nobody knew the danger. Two men buried the paperwork to avoid an inspection. The widow gets the money. Nobody profits. But the mechanism — bury the truth, protect the village from outsiders — is exactly the same mechanism that buried the lynch. Players will feel like they're pulling the same thread. They're not. But the shape of the lie is identical.

## Michał Pytlak's Reaction When Pressed

- **Stage 1 — Deflection.** "Mazur? He's... not been around lately. Seasonal work, you know how it is." Awkward, not convincing.
- **Stage 2 — Distress.** If players push — especially if they mention talking to Wanda — he goes pale. Stops working. Asks to speak privately. He's not angry, he's afraid. Not of punishment — of Wanda finding out her pension isn't real.
- **Stage 3 — Runs to Zbigniew Gajda.** Not to confess — to warn him. *"They know about Mazur. They've been to the farm, they've talked to the widow."* Zbigniew Gajda hears "they know" and his mind goes straight to the lynch. Michał Pytlak thinks it's about the unreported death. Neither understands the other's fear. Both men act guilty and evasive — which makes the players think they've found the big secret.

## Connections to Larger Story

- **Zbigniew Gajda knows.** He ordered it. His motivation was partly protecting the farm, partly protecting the village from any outside scrutiny. When the committee starts pulling this thread, his paranoia spikes — because the same logic that hid a farm death also hid a murder.
- **Michał Pytlak doesn't know about the lynch.** He arrived in %NEW_VILLAGE% after 1954. His guilt is about negligence and a lie, not about the village's deeper crimes. He has zero information about the core mystery.
- **Wanda doesn't know.** She believes she receives a state pension. If she ever learns the truth — that her husband's death was covered up, that he officially never died, that her "pension" is phantom wages — it would destroy her. Michał Pytlak and Zbigniew Gajda both fear this more than any state investigation.
- **The village doesn't know.** They think it was all handled properly. If it comes out, it doesn't create a village crisis — it creates a crisis for two men.

## For the GM

The cover-up is a lever with multiple uses:
- **Thematic echo:** The village buries its dead and its guilt together. This is the lynch in miniature — same mechanism, smaller stakes. Players who crack this thread will recognise the pattern when they find the bigger one.
- **Paranoia amplifier:** Michał Pytlak running to Zbigniew Gajda makes the sołtys tighten up — he thinks the committee knows more than they do. Miscommunication between two men with two different secrets.
- **Moral complexity:** If players discover it, what do they do? Report it? That destroys Wanda's income. Keep quiet? They're now part of the cover-up. Use it as leverage? Over a man who got someone killed and has been quietly paying for it ever since.
- **Not a dead end.** This thread *feels* connected to the main mystery — the shape of the lie is the same. Players who follow it learn how this village operates: bury the truth, keep outsiders away, pay whatever it costs in silence.
