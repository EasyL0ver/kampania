# Zofia Pytlak

**Type:** Named character — PGR cook / the village's warmth

## Vital Statistics

- **Born:** ~1920
- **Age in 1967:** ~47
- **Heritage:** Polish settler — arrived with Michał Pytlak after 1954. No connection to the lynch.
- **Family:** Married to [Michał Pytlak](foreman.md) (farm overseer). Mother of [Staszek Pytlak](secondary-characters.md) (age 5).
- **Lives in:** Pytlak household, near the PGR farm.
- **Settled:** After 1954 — arrived with Michał Pytlak

## Character

Zofia is the warmest person in %NEW_VILLAGE%. She feeds people — workers, guests, children, anyone who comes near her kitchen. She's loud in a kind way, generous without thinking about it, genuinely happy in a village full of people pretending to be. She beams when complimented. She sets an extra plate without being asked when Tadek Gajda stumbles in drunk.

She is the hearth. The social glue. The reason the dinner scene feels human and the farm feels like a workplace instead of a prison. When Zofia is in a room, people relax.

## The Silo — What She Knows

Zofia knows **everything** about the silo cover-up. Michał came home that day shaking, covered in grain dust, unable to speak. She got the truth out of him that night — not from Wojewoda, from her husband. He told her because he couldn't carry it alone.

She knows Tadeusz died because Michał sent him in. She knows Michał went in after him and nearly died himself. She knows the death was never reported. She knows Wanda Mazur's "pension" is phantom wages. She knows Wojewoda ordered the cover-up to keep inspectors away.

**She wants him to report it.** She's been saying it for two years: *"Tell the truth. Face whatever comes. Stop living like this."* Her "let go" is specific — let go of the lie, let go of the silence, let go of Wojewoda's grip on you. Accept the consequences and be free.

He can't. Wojewoda won't allow it. And Michał is loyal — to the village, to his boss, to the arrangement that keeps Wanda fed. So the guilt stays, the lie stays, and Zofia watches her husband rot from the inside.

**For players:** If the committee is kind to her and gains her trust, she may tell them about the silo. Not out of betrayal — she's been desperate for someone to listen for two years. She wants the truth out. She wants her husband unburdened. She's a **potential source** for the cover-up, and the easiest one to reach — she'll talk if someone just asks and listens.

**Mirror to Irena — wanting truth is easy when you don't know what it costs.** Zofia knows a small truth (the silo) and wants it told. The cost is manageable — shame, maybe punishment. She pushes for honesty. Irena spends 13 years hunting a truth she doesn't have. When she finally gets it — rape, murder, infanticide, her husband's hands — she wants to bury it. Because the cost is everything: her family, her son, her life. The woman who searched for truth becomes its enemy. The woman who's known truth all along is the one saying "tell."

## The Pattern — "Let Go"

This is the phrase that defines Zofia's relationship with Michał:

- **After the silo (~1965):** *"It wasn't your fault. Report it. Tell the truth. Let go."*
- **During the flood (Act II):** *"Come home. Stop. Let the water come. Let go."*
- **She dies (Act III):** The voice saying "let go" goes silent. Nobody is left to say it.
- **If players intervene:** They become Zofia's voice. They say what she can't anymore.

## Role in the Story

### Act I — Joy

Zofia is the village at its best. She cooks for the committee's first dinner. She serves with warmth, she makes the government people feel welcome. Workers talk freely around her — she hears everything but doesn't weaponize it. She's an open door. Ask her anything and she'll answer honestly, not because she's naive but because she sees no reason to lie.

**For players:** She's an accidental information source. She doesn't know what's dangerous to say. She'll mention who's been acting strange, who stopped eating, who's been arguing. She does this because she cares, not because she's a gossip.

### Act II — Doubt

The flood hits. [Michał Pytlak](foreman.md) throws himself into the fight — sandbags, ditches, barriers. He's 57 years old. He should be tired. He's not. He doesn't come home. He doesn't eat. He doesn't sleep. He works through the night, hauls loads men half his age can't lift, stands in floodwater for hours and doesn't shiver.

**Zofia is the first to see it.** She's his wife. She knows his body — the bad knee, the sore back, the way he breathes after heavy work. None of that is happening anymore. He moves wrong. He's too strong. His eyes don't focus on her the way they used to.

She tries to tell people. Nobody listens. *"Your husband is saving the village, Zofia. You should be proud."* She tries to pull him home. He barely registers her. She brings him food at the sandbags. He doesn't eat it.

She starts pleading: **stop. Come home. Rest. Let the water come. We'll start over somewhere else. Please.** He can't hear her. The well won't let him stop.

### Act III — Gone

Zofia stops cooking. The kitchen goes cold. Nobody notices immediately — the flood, the crisis, everything else. [Staszek](secondary-characters.md) is staying with another family because the Pytlak house is too close to the water line.

She's alone in the house. Her husband is somewhere in the dark hauling sandbags with hands that don't bleed anymore. Her son is elsewhere. The village she fed and warmed is tearing itself apart.

**Zofia kills herself.** Quietly. In the house. Nobody finds her until it's too late — because the one person who would have noticed is out saving the village with strength that isn't his.

The man who fought hardest to protect %NEW_VILLAGE% couldn't protect the person in his own kitchen.

## The Well's Influence

The well has no guilt to amplify in Zofia — she arrived after the crimes. Instead, it takes. It drains her warmth the way it drains the land of stability. The effect is gradual:

- First she's tired. Normal tired — it's a crisis, everyone's tired.
- Then she's flat. The smile is gone but she's still functional. Still cooking, still working.
- Then she's empty. Food burns. Plates unwashed. She sits and stares. The light behind her eyes is off.
- Then she's gone.

The well's cruelest trick: it gave her husband inhuman strength to keep him away from the one person who could have saved him from its grip. Zofia was the voice saying *let go* — and the well made sure he couldn't hear it.

## Thematic Role — "The Village Doesn't Deserve It"

Zofia is the **price tag** of the village's survival. Every ending where %NEW_VILLAGE% continues — the flood is fought, the report is written, life goes on — has to contend with her empty kitchen.

The Foreman saves the village? His wife died alone so he could haul sandbags for a community built on a mass grave. The committee writes a favorable report? The village gets to keep existing, keep burying, keep consuming its own. Whatever the players achieve, Zofia is the question: **was this village worth what it cost?**

She's the moral argument that some things shouldn't be saved.

## The Choice — Save Zofia or Save the Village

Players who notice Zofia's decline can intervene. If they take her seriously — sit with her, listen, help her confront Michał — they can break the well's grip on the Foreman. He comes home. He stops. He's human again.

**But without his inhuman strength, the flood defense collapses.** He's the only one holding it together. The drainage fails. The water wins. Homes are lost. Maybe lives.

| Choice | Zofia | Foreman | Village | Ending |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Help Zofia | Lives | Comes back, human | Floods — defense collapses | "Foreman saves village" ending **lost** |
| Ignore / don't notice | Dies | Well keeps him, breaks him after | Survives the flood | "Foreman saves village" ending **available** — but at what cost? |

The players don't know the stakes when Zofia first asks for help. They just see a tired woman who says something is wrong with her husband.

## For the GM

**Zofia's arc is a barometer.** Her state tells the players how far the village has fallen:

| Zofia's state | What it means |
|---|---|
| Warm, cooking, beaming | Village is still holding together. Act I. |
| Tired but functional | Stress is real but manageable. Early Act II. |
| Quiet, flat, going through motions | Something is wrong. Mid Act II. |
| Kitchen cold, not cooking | The village's warmth is dying. Late Act II. |
| Gone | The village has consumed its own heart. Act III. |

**Preventable?** Yes — if the players notice and intervene. See "The Choice" above.

**Content warning:** Her death should be flagged in session zero. The GM can adjust the severity — she can break without dying, or disappear, or simply shut down. The suicide is the hardest version. Use it only with a group that's prepared for it.

## Relationships

- **[Michał Pytlak](foreman.md)** — Her husband. She loves him completely. She knows about the silo — he told her that night because he couldn't carry it alone. She's been pushing him to confess for two years. Now she's watching him become something inhuman and she can't reach him. The tragedy: she's the only person who could save him, and the well's gift is designed to keep her away.
- **[Staszek Pytlak](secondary-characters.md)** — Her son. Her reason. When the well takes her warmth, the last thing to go is her concern for him — she makes sure he's safe with another family before she goes.
- **[Irena Gajda](wife.md)** — Connected through the household. Irena is calculating where Zofia is open. Thematic mirror: Zofia knows a small secret and wants it told; Irena hunts a huge one for 13 years and buries it the moment she finds it. Wanting truth is easy when you don't know what it costs.
- **[Barbara Kopacz](barbara.md)** — Works on the farm. Zofia is kind to her without pity. One of the few people who treats Barbara as a person, not a problem.
- **[Wanda Mazur](widow.md)** — Both are PGR wives. Zofia knows the truth about Wanda's "pension" — that it's phantom wages, not a state benefit. She's kind to Wanda and it costs her every time, knowing what she knows.

## Open Questions

- Does Staszek understand what happened?
- Could the players have prevented it if they'd paid attention to her instead of the mystery?
- How does Michał react — does the well's grip break when she dies, or does it tighten?
