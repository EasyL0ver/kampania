# %PAINTER%

**Type:** Named character

## Vital Statistics

- **Born:** 1923
- **Age in 1967:** 44

## Role

Fiancé of %MATRONA%. Was secretly romancing %JAGNA% (daughter of %SOLDIER%).

## Relationships

- **%MATRONA%** — wife (married after the lynch, despite the affair)
- **%JAGNA%** — secret lover (now dead)

## The Lynch

Caught in flagranti with %JAGNA% by %MATRONA%. Beaten by %WUJAS%, %WOJEWODA%, and %BUTCHER% but **spared** — chased away on account of being %MATRONA%'s fiancé.

## In 1967

Married to %MATRONA%. Living in %NEW_VILLAGE%. The man whose affair started everything — and who was beaten and chased off while %JAGNA% was murdered behind him. Does he know what happened after he left? Does he live with the guilt of surviving when she didn't?

## The Flood & The Committee

**He secretly wants %JAGNA% in the census.** He won't say it. He can't say it — %MATRONA% would destroy him. But when the government people start writing names and counting souls, something in him wants to walk up to them and say: *there was a woman named %JAGNA%. She lived here. Write her down.*

The census is the last record before the flood erases this place. If %JAGNA% isn't on paper, she never existed. The village drowns and she drowns with it a second time. %PAINTER% can't bear that — and he can't do anything about it. Not while %MATRONA% is watching.

If the players get him alone — truly alone, away from her — this is what comes out. Not a confession about the lynch. A name. He wants a dead woman written on a piece of government paper so that something survives the water.

## The Well's Influence

%PAINTER% is mostly innocent — a man caught in the wrong place whose life was destroyed by others' violence. The well doesn't torment him the way it torments the killers. It reaches him the only way it can: **through his art.**

He starts painting the well. Not deliberately — it appears in his work uninvited. A dark circle in the background of a landscape. A ring of stones in a portrait that shouldn't have one. The water in his paintings turns black. He doesn't notice at first. When he does, he can't stop.

The paintings are the well's way of being seen by someone with no guilt to amplify and no secret to crack — just a pair of hands that know how to make the invisible visible. If the players find his paintings, they'll see the well before they find it.
