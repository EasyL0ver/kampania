# %PRIEST%

**Type:** Named character — village priest

## Vital Statistics

- **Born:** 1925
- **Age in 1967:** 42

## Role

Roman Catholic priest residing at the small church in %NEW_VILLAGE%. Spiritual authority of the community.

## What He Knows

%PRIEST% knows **far more than he lets on.** Through 13 years of confessions, observation, and careful listening, he has pieced together a near-complete picture — and the key source is **%CIOTKA%**.

%WOJEWODA%'s confessions are guarded — he gives fragments wrapped in justification. %MATRONA%'s are masterful misdirection — real guilt carefully framed to hide her authorship. But %CIOTKA% is too honest, too broken, and too desperate for absolution to edit herself. Over 13 years of confessions, she has told him **everything** — not deliberately, not all at once, but piece by piece, year by year, the whole picture spilled out. The rape, the killings, who did what, where the bodies went. She couldn't hold it alone.

She doesn't realize how much she's given him. She doesn't think of confession as testimony — it's the one place she can breathe. %PRIEST% has never told her how much he's assembled from her words.

**What he knows:**
- **The lynch (1954):** The full sequence — %MATRONA%'s orchestration, the assault on %JAGNA%, the killings, %BUTCHER% strangling %GLUPEK%, %CIOTKA% saving him. He knows who did what.
- **%GLUPEK%'s origin:** The boy is %SOLDIER%'s surviving son. His brain damage was inflicted, not congenital.
- **The well:** Where the bodies were dumped — the same well that holds the Lemko massacre remains.
- **The village's silence:** How the whole community absorbed a family's disappearance without a question.

**%WUJAS% does not confess** — the alcoholic has abandoned the church.

## His Choice: Silence

%PRIEST% has chosen to **suppress what he knows.** Not out of malice, not out of cowardice — out of a genuine, deeply held belief that exposing the truth would cause more harm than burying it.

His reasoning:
- **The seal of confession is absolute.** Even if he wanted to speak, his faith forbids it. But this goes beyond the sacramental seal — he has actively decided the truth should stay hidden.
- **The village would be destroyed.** Arrests, trials, families torn apart, children left without parents. %CIOTKA% — the only good person in this — would go down with the rest. %GLUPEK% would lose his caretaker.
- **Nothing can be undone.** The dead are dead. Justice won't bring them back. What it will bring is more suffering piled on top of old suffering.
- **He can do more good from inside.** By staying silent, he keeps his position — and his position lets him protect %GLUPEK%, counsel the guilty, and hold the village together. If he speaks, he loses everything and helps no one.

He's not wrong about any of this. That's what makes him dangerous.

## The Siblings' Generosity

The church in %NEW_VILLAGE% is suspiciously well-maintained for a poor frontier parish. The roof doesn't leak. The altar has decent candles. %PRIEST% eats better than most villagers.

**The siblings fund him.** %WOJEWODA% provides from the PGR — firewood, food, occasional repairs. %MATRONA% donates through the store — supplies at no cost, gifts for the church. Even %CIOTKA% contributes what little she has. It's never framed as a bribe. It's framed as piety — a devout family supporting their parish.

But the effect is the same. %PRIEST%'s material comfort depends on the family whose sins he keeps. He didn't ask for this arrangement and he didn't negotiate it — it grew organically, the way these things do. The siblings give because giving to the church is what good Catholics do. That it also makes the priest dependent on their goodwill is something nobody says out loud.

**Does %PRIEST% see it?** Almost certainly. He's not stupid. But acknowledging the dynamic would mean acknowledging that his silence has a price tag — and that price is being paid. It's easier to see the donations as genuine faith and his silence as genuine theology. Both might even be true. But they're also convenient.

**For the players:** This is the crack in %PRIEST%'s moral armor. His arguments for silence are philosophical and sincere. But the firewood and the candles and the full pantry — those are material. If the players discover the funding pattern, they can reframe his noble silence as something uglier: a man being paid to look away. Whether that's fair or not is beside the point. It's leverage.

## Relationship to %GLUPEK%

**A genuine protector.** %PRIEST% cares for %GLUPEK% deeply — alongside %CIOTKA%, he's the boy's strongest shield. The church is %GLUPEK%'s safe space, the one place where even %BUTCHER% wouldn't touch him. %PRIEST% teaches him small tasks, gives him purpose, treats him with patience and dignity.

This is not performance. %PRIEST% truly believes that protecting %GLUPEK% is the best he can do for the boy — better than any justice system could offer. And he may be right. But it's also the thing that lets him sleep at night. *I protect the survivor, therefore my silence is mercy, not complicity.*

## Relationship to %WIFE%

**An adversary.** %WIFE% has approached %PRIEST% — obliquely, carefully — more than once over the years. She's come to confession and talked around her suspicions. She's asked him directly: *"Father, do you think something happened to that family?"* She's watched his face for a reaction.

%PRIEST% has shut her down every time. Gently, pastorally, firmly:
- *"Some things are between a person and God."*
- *"Suspicion without proof is a sin against your neighbour."*
- *"Your duty is to your family and your faith, not to questions that have no answers."*

He's not cruel about it. He genuinely believes he's protecting her — from the truth, from the consequences of pursuing it, from her husband's reaction if he finds out she's asking. But the effect is the same: the one authority figure she might have trusted has told her, repeatedly, to stop looking.

**%WIFE% sees him as a gatekeeper of the silence.** She doesn't know how much he knows, but she knows he's chosen to stand with the quiet. This is why she never fully trusted institutional authority — not the state, not the church. Both told her to shut up.

When the committee arrives, %PRIEST% will try the same approach on the players: redirect, counsel patience, warn against stirring up old pain. If they push, he'll frame their investigation as reckless — outsiders who don't understand what this village needs. He'll use %GLUPEK% as a shield: *"Think of what this will do to the boy."*

## In 1967

A decent man making a terrible bargain. He protects %GLUPEK%, counsels the guilty, holds the village together — and in doing so, ensures that murderers live free and a woman's search for truth is smothered by pastoral concern.

**%PRIEST% arrived in %NEW_VILLAGE% after the lynch.** He wasn't there when it happened. Everything he knows comes from confessions, observation, and the village's uneasy silences. He's had 13 years to assemble the picture — and 13 years to decide what to do with it.

He chose nothing. He chose stability. He chose the living over the dead.

For the players, %PRIEST% is maddening — because he's not wrong about the cost of truth, and he's not pretending to be something he's not. He's a good man who decided that goodness means keeping the peace. The question the players must answer: **is he right?**

## The Flood & The Committee

**%PRIEST% is unfriendly to the committee by default.** Government people. Communists, probably. Men with clipboards and no reverence. He's polite — he's a priest — but cold. Unhelpful. Answers in one sentence. Doesn't invite them in.

**The key to unlocking him is faith — or at least respect for it.** If a player demonstrates genuine belief, knowledge of scripture, respect for the Ten Commandments, or simply treats his church and his role with sincerity — %PRIEST% opens. Not all the way, but enough. He'll talk. He'll offer tea. He'll share his observations about the village — carefully, selectively, testing whether these people deserve more.

Without that respect, the players get nothing from him. He sees them as another wave of state apparatus — the same power that destroyed the Lemkos, that sent %OFFICER%, that built the dam. Why would he help them?

**The danger: %PRIEST% can call a lynch on the players.** If the committee pushes too hard, too publicly, without building trust — if they threaten the village's fragile stability and %PRIEST% decides they're a greater danger than the secrets he keeps — he can turn the village against them. Not with a command. With a sermon. A word to the right people. A suggestion that these outsiders are here to destroy, not help. In a flooded village with no road out, a priest who decides the committee is the enemy is the most dangerous man alive. The same authority that keeps the peace can break it.

**He can also be weaponized by %MATRONA%.** If she feels exposed — if the players are getting too close to the lynch, to her role in it — she can go to %PRIEST% and frame the committee as a threat to the village. %PRIEST% trusts her piety (she's his unwitting masterpiece). If %MATRONA% says these people are dangerous, %PRIEST% has 13 years of reasons to believe her. He may call a lynch not to protect himself, but to protect the village from a threat that %MATRONA% manufactured.

## The Well's Influence

The well erodes %PRIEST%'s **theological certainty** — the foundation his entire 13-year bargain rests on.

- His prayers feel hollow. The words come out but nothing answers. Mass feels like performance. The confessional — where he's sat for 13 years absorbing the village's sins — starts to feel like a trap, not a sacrament.
- He becomes irritable, short with parishioners, distracted during services. %KRYSTIAN% notices. %MATRONA% notices. The warm, steady pastor is fraying.
- The dreams come. Not about the dead — about the well itself. Stone walls descending into darkness. Water rising. The sound of prayer in a language that isn't Latin and isn't Polish. He wakes up knowing that something is down there that his God doesn't govern.
- His 13-year justification — *"I chose the living over the dead"* — starts to collapse from within. The dead aren't staying dead. The living aren't staying sane. The bargain he made isn't holding, and the faith that underwrote it is going silent.

The well doesn't break %PRIEST% with guilt — he's not a perpetrator. It breaks him with **irrelevance.** His prayers aren't working. His silence isn't protecting anyone. Something older and deeper than his theology is moving under the village, and he has no tools to address it. The only tools that work belong to a tradition he's spent his career dismissing.

This is what eventually makes him grant permission for the Lemko rite. Not a change of heart — a collapse of certainty. See "The Ritual — The Final Bottleneck" section.

## Relationships

### %MATRONA% — Fondness, shared piety

%PRIEST%'s closest relationship among the siblings. %MATRONA% is **deeply, performatively, and possibly genuinely religious**. She attends every service, organizes church events, placed her son %KRYSTIAN% as altar boy. She and %PRIEST% have long conversations about faith, morality, the parish. He sees in her a woman of conviction — pious, strong, devoted to her family.

He doesn't see the architect of a murder. %MATRONA%'s confessions are the most carefully constructed — she brings him real guilt wrapped in misdirection, and he receives it as a soul struggling honestly with sin. Their rapport is warm, intellectual, almost a friendship. She's the parishioner he'd point to as proof that faith works.

**This is %MATRONA%'s masterpiece.** She hasn't just fooled him — she's made him her ally without him knowing it. When players start asking about the siblings, %PRIEST% will instinctively defend %MATRONA% first. *"A woman of deep faith. Whatever happened in this village, she has suffered for it."*

### %WOJEWODA% — Frenemy, mutual balance

A pragmatic relationship built on coexistence. %WOJEWODA% controls the village politically; %PRIEST% controls it spiritually. They need each other and they know it. There's mutual respect — but also friction. %WOJEWODA% sees the church as useful for keeping order. %PRIEST% sees the sołtys as a necessary brute.

They share meals, discuss village business, negotiate small disputes together. It works. But underneath there's an edge — %PRIEST% knows what kind of man %WOJEWODA% is, and %WOJEWODA% knows the priest hears things in the confessional. Neither fully trusts the other. Both prefer this careful balance to the alternative.

The siblings' funding flows primarily through %WOJEWODA% — which means the power dynamic tilts slightly his way. %PRIEST% is aware of this tilt without admitting it affects him.

### %OFFICER% — Antagonism

**%OFFICER% despises what %PRIEST% represents.** As a true believer in the communist project, he sees the Church as a relic — superstition, backwardness, a rival power structure that the state will eventually replace. In Bieszczady, where the state's grip is weak, a village priest holds real influence, and that grates.

%OFFICER% has made his contempt clear through small cruelties: paperwork complications, veiled threats about the church's "unsanctioned activities," reminders that the state tolerates religion only as long as it stays quiet. He'd love a reason to shut the parish down.

**%PRIEST% fears %OFFICER%.** Not personally — spiritually. %OFFICER% represents the machinery that could strip him of his church, his parish, his purpose. This fear reinforces his silence: if %PRIEST% makes trouble, %OFFICER% won't just bury the truth — he'll bury the priest's entire life along with it.

**The irony:** On the question of the village's secrets, they're aligned — both want silence, both want stability. But they can't cooperate because %OFFICER% sees the priest as an enemy of the state, and %PRIEST% sees the officer as an enemy of God. Two men pulling in the same direction while hating each other. The players can potentially exploit this — driving a wedge between allies who don't even know they're allies.

## The Ritual — The Final Bottleneck

After %HAG%'s death, the players may discover the path to performing the Lemko rite at the well (see `story-facts\the-ritual.md`). They need %BABCIA% for the words. %BABCIA% needs %BARBARA% to physically bring her there — she's too frail to cross the forest alone.

**%BARBARA% won't do it without %PRIEST%'s permission.** She's a devout Catholic. Taking her elderly relative into the rain to perform a Lemko pagan rite at a haunted well — that's heresy. She's poor, dependent, and scared. The one authority she trusts outside %WOJEWODA%'s household is %PRIEST%. If he says no, she says no. If he says nothing, she says no. He must actively, explicitly give his blessing.

**This is the hardest thing the story asks of %PRIEST%.** Not silence — he's good at silence. Not protection — he protects %GLUPEK% every day. The story asks him to endorse something his faith condemns: a non-Christian rite, performed by a non-Catholic woman, for dead souls outside his church's authority. It asks him to admit that his 13 years of bargaining with God weren't enough — that the dead need something he can't provide.

### How the players break him

%PRIEST% resists by default. His theology, his comfort, his entire 13-year architecture of silence tells him to say no. The players must give him a reason stronger than all of that:

- **The dreams.** After %HAG%'s death, %PRIEST% starts dreaming too. The well reaches him. A man who's spent 13 years managing his conscience through prayer suddenly can't sleep, can't pray, can't think. His God isn't answering. The well is.
- **%GLUPEK% in danger.** If the village's deterioration threatens %GLUPEK% — the boy he's protected for 13 years — %PRIEST%'s priorities shift. The rite isn't about theology anymore. It's about saving the boy.
- **The truth laid bare.** If the players confront %PRIEST% with what they know — that he's known about the lynch all along, that %CIOTKA% told him everything, that his silence protected murderers — the moral architecture collapses. He can't be the good man who chose peace if peace was always a lie.
- **%BABCIA% herself.** If the players bring %PRIEST% to %BABCIA% — or %BABCIA% to %PRIEST% — and the old woman speaks to him in Lemko about the dead, about the prayers, about what she heard on the wind and what's gone silent now — it may break him at a level theology can't reach. Two people of faith, from two traditions, both knowing the dead need tending.

### What his permission looks like

Not enthusiasm. Not conversion. A beaten man saying: *"Go. Take her. Do what I cannot."*

He may come with them. He may stay behind. But the permission is what matters — it frees %BARBARA%, which frees %BABCIA%, which makes the ritual possible.

### What it costs him

Everything he built. His alliance with %MATRONA%. His balance with %WOJEWODA%. His funded comfort. His theological certainty. His 13-year bet that silence was mercy. By saying yes to the ritual, he's saying: *I was wrong. The dead needed more than I gave them. Someone else has to finish what I couldn't start.*
