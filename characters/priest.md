# ks. Władysław Pająk

**Type:** Named character — village priest

## Vital Statistics

- **Born:** 1925
- **Age in 1967:** 42
- **Settled:** After 1954 — assigned to the parish after the lynch

## Character

Roman Catholic priest, arrived after the lynch. Knows everything through 13 years of confessions — primarily from Janina. Has chosen silence. The final bottleneck for the Lemko ritual.

## Relationships

### Helena Rzepka — Fondness, shared piety

ks. Władysław Pająk's closest relationship among the siblings. Helena Rzepka is **deeply, performatively, and possibly genuinely religious**. She attends every service, organizes church events, placed her son Krystian Rzepka as altar boy. She and ks. Władysław Pająk have long conversations about faith, morality, the parish. He sees in her a woman of conviction — pious, strong, devoted to her family.

He doesn't see the architect of a murder. Helena Rzepka's confessions are the most carefully constructed — she brings him real guilt wrapped in misdirection, and he receives it as a soul struggling honestly with sin. Their rapport is warm, intellectual, almost a friendship. She's the parishioner he'd point to as proof that faith works.

**This is Helena Rzepka's masterpiece.** She hasn't just fooled him — she's made him her ally without him knowing it. When players start asking about the siblings, ks. Władysław Pająk will instinctively defend Helena Rzepka first. *"A woman of deep faith. Whatever happened in this village, she has suffered for it."*

### Zbigniew Gajda — Frenemy, mutual balance

A pragmatic relationship built on coexistence. Zbigniew Gajda controls the village politically; ks. Władysław Pająk controls it spiritually. They need each other and they know it. There's mutual respect — but also friction. Zbigniew Gajda sees the church as useful for keeping order. ks. Władysław Pająk sees the sołtys as a necessary brute.

They share meals, discuss village business, negotiate small disputes together. It works. But underneath there's an edge — ks. Władysław Pająk knows what kind of man Zbigniew Gajda is, and Zbigniew Gajda knows the priest hears things in the confessional. Neither fully trusts the other. Both prefer this careful balance to the alternative.

The siblings' funding flows primarily through Zbigniew Gajda — which means the power dynamic tilts slightly his way. ks. Władysław Pająk is aware of this tilt without admitting it affects him.

### por. Witold Skowron — Antagonism

**por. Witold Skowron despises what ks. Władysław Pająk represents.** As a true believer in the communist project, he sees the Church as a relic — superstition, backwardness, a rival power structure that the state will eventually replace. In Bieszczady, where the state's grip is weak, a village priest holds real influence, and that grates.

por. Witold Skowron has made his contempt clear through small cruelties: paperwork complications, veiled threats about the church's "unsanctioned activities," reminders that the state tolerates religion only as long as it stays quiet. He'd love a reason to shut the parish down.

**ks. Władysław Pająk fears por. Witold Skowron.** Not personally — spiritually. por. Witold Skowron represents the machinery that could strip him of his church, his parish, his purpose. This fear reinforces his silence: if ks. Władysław Pająk makes trouble, por. Witold Skowron won't just bury the truth — he'll bury the priest's entire life along with it.

**The irony:** On the question of the village's secrets, they're aligned — both want silence, both want stability. But they can't cooperate because por. Witold Skowron sees the priest as an enemy of the state, and ks. Władysław Pająk sees the officer as an enemy of God. Two men pulling in the same direction while hating each other. The players can potentially exploit this — driving a wedge between allies who don't even know they're allies.
