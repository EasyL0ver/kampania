# %BUTCHER%

**Type:** Named character — symbolic Lemko rage

## Vital Statistics

- **Born:** 1921
- **Age in 1967:** 46

## Heritage

**Not Lemko.** Polish settler, like most of %NEW_VILLAGE%. He has no ancestral connection to %OLD_VILLAGE% or the Lemko people. That's what makes his obsession so strange.

## Role

**The rage of the Lemkos incarnate — channeled through a man who isn't one of them.** %BUTCHER% is a sociopath. He's violent, cold, and dangerous. But there's something else in him — an irrational, bone-deep fixation with %OLD_VILLAGE% and the well that has no logical explanation. He doesn't understand it himself. He can't name it. But it drives him back to the ruins again and again, as if something down there is calling to him.

Symbolically, he carries the unprocessed fury of the Lemko dead — their rage at being massacred, erased, forgotten — expressed through the worst possible vessel. A man with no empathy, no heritage, no right to the grief. But the grief found him anyway.

## The Lynch

One of the three drunk men who attacked %JAGNA%. **Participated in the rape** along with %WUJAS%. After %SOLDIER% was killed, it was %BUTCHER% who said the wife and baby had to die too — no witnesses. %MATRONA% gave the order, but %BUTCHER% didn't need convincing. He carried it out with cold efficiency.

He was the one who insisted on the well. Not just as a disposal site — as **the** place. Something about it felt right in a way he couldn't articulate. He didn't know the Lemko massacre victims were already down there. The choice wasn't rational. It was gravitational.

## The Obsession

%BUTCHER% visits %OLD_VILLAGE% regularly. Not with ritual or purpose — he just **goes there**. Sits near the well. Walks the ruins. Sometimes for hours. The dogs come with him but won't go near the well — they stop at a perimeter and refuse to cross it. He crosses it every time.

He doesn't tend the graves. He doesn't light candles. He's not mourning — he doesn't have the emotional architecture for mourning. But he's *present*. Drawn to a place of death by something he can't name. A gravity.

**What he does at the well:** He watches it. He's cleared debris from it more than once — not to expose evidence, not to hide it, just to keep it... open. Accessible. Like it matters that the well stays visible. If the water rises and drowns it, something in him will break — or explode.

**The dreams drive him.** %BUTCHER% has been having dreams about the well for years — the same kind of dreams the players will start having when they arrive. He sleeps badly. The dogs whine at night, hearing him thrash and mumble. He's been seen walking toward %OLD_VILLAGE% at night, half-dressed, barefoot — returning before dawn. He clears debris from the well because in the dreams, it's always blocked and the dead can't get out. He doesn't talk about any of this. See `story-facts\the-well.md` for the full supernatural element.

**He doesn't know about the 1947 massacre victims in the well.** He put the lynch victims there because the place pulled him. The fact that Lemko dead were already inside — that his victims joined theirs — is a coincidence that isn't a coincidence. It's the story's deepest symbolic rhyme, and no character in the narrative is aware of it.

## Relationships

### %GLUPEK% — Unfinished business

%BUTCHER% watches %GLUPEK% the way a man watches a mistake he can't correct. The boy survived — that was %CIOTKA%'s doing — and now he walks the village as a 17-year-old, six-foot reminder that the job wasn't finished. Whether %BUTCHER% would act on this if the situation demanded it is an open question. The answer is probably yes.

### %CIOTKA% — The woman who defied him

She stopped him from killing the infant. He has never forgiven it. %CIOTKA% is the only person in the village who physically intervened against him, and %BUTCHER% remembers every person who ever crossed him. They don't speak. The silence between them is louder than anything else in the village.

### %WOJEWODA% and %WUJAS% — Co-conspirators, decayed bond

They did it together. That bond should hold them close — instead it rotted. %WOJEWODA% keeps him at arm's length; %WUJAS% drinks to forget him. %BUTCHER% lives alone on the village edge, useful as a threat but unwanted as a neighbour. He knows this. He accepts it with the cold patience of a man who knows where the bodies are.

### %HAG% — The unseen opposition

%BUTCHER% doesn't know %HAG% exists. He doesn't know anyone else visits %OLD_VILLAGE%. But he's noticed things — debris re-piled on the well after he cleared it, candle wax in the cerkiew, the faint smell of incense on nights when nobody should be there. He chalks it up to the ruins being strange. He's right — but not in the way he thinks. Someone is fighting him for the well, and he doesn't know it.

## In 1967

Lives on the **edge of the village** with his dogs. An **outcast** — the village either knows or suspects what he did during the lynch. He's not exiled, but he's not welcome either. People avoid him.

**And he avoids them.** This is the detail that complicates the monster. %BUTCHER% has structured his entire life to **minimise harm.** He doesn't drink — he saw what alcohol did that night in 1954, how it unlocked the three of them, and he's never touched it since. He doesn't socialise. He doesn't attend village gatherings, doesn't go to the store when it's busy, doesn't linger where people are. He lives at the edge, keeps to his dogs, does his work, and stays away.

He's not doing this out of guilt — he doesn't have the emotional wiring for guilt. He's doing it out of **self-knowledge.** He knows what he is. He knows what he's capable of. He felt the ease of it in 1954 — how natural the violence was, how little it cost him. That scared him in the only way a sociopath can be scared: not morally, but practically. He understood that if he stayed around people, around drink, around friction — it would happen again. So he removed himself.

The isolation, the dogs, the avoidance — it's not punishment. It's **containment.** His own private version of what %HAG% does at the well. He's trying to keep something dangerous from getting out.

**But the compulsion is winning.** The well's pull is stronger than his self-imposed discipline. As the water rises, the carefully constructed walls around his behaviour erode — and the erosion is visible to the whole village:

- **He starts showing up.** At the store. Near the farm. Lingering where people gather. The man who avoided everyone for 13 years is suddenly *around*. Villagers notice. It's unsettling — like a guard dog that's slipped its chain and is walking calmly through the house.
- **He starts drinking.** The one rule he never broke. He shows up at the bimber still, or accepts a bottle from %WUJAS%'s crew. The men who avoided him don't know how to refuse — and they're too drunk or too scared to try. %BUTCHER% drinking is the village's canary in the coal mine. Everyone who remembers 1954 knows what alcohol did to those men. Seeing %BUTCHER% with a bottle should terrify anyone paying attention.
- **He's not aggressive — yet.** That's what makes it worse. He's calm, almost pleasant. Makes small talk he's never made before. The sociopath is performing normalcy, and the performance is flawless because there's nothing underneath it competing. The self-knowledge that kept him away is gone. He doesn't know why he's here. He just knows the edge of the village doesn't feel right anymore. The centre does.
- **The dogs stay at the edge.** They won't follow him into the village. They pace the perimeter of his property and whine. Even they can tell something is wrong with him.

The water and the compulsion are the same curve. The man who contained himself for 13 years is uncontaining — and everyone can see it happening in slow motion.

**If the committee starts poking around %OLD_VILLAGE%**, %BUTCHER% will react. Not to protect evidence — he doesn't care about the cover-up the way %WOJEWODA% does. He'll react because **they're trespassing on something that's his** in a way he can't articulate and they can't understand. The well, the ruins, the dead — they belong to him. Not legally. Not morally. But in his gut.

**He may kill %HAG%.** If the rising pressure drives both of them to the well on the same night — and nothing intervenes — %BUTCHER% will find the woman who's been undoing his work for 20 years and put her in the well. The compulsion completing another cycle, then demanding more. But this is **not guaranteed** — the players can prevent it. See `story\event-butcher-kills-hag.md`.

## The Flood & The Committee

**%BUTCHER% doesn't care about the committee.** Government people, census, evacuation plans — none of it touches him. He has no property worth documenting, no family to relocate, no stake in the village's future.

**He's looking forward to the flood.** Not in a calculated way like %WOJEWODA%. In a visceral, inarticulate way. The water is coming to the well. The water is going to fill it, cover it, swallow it. The thing that's been pulling at him for 13 years is about to be drowned — or completed — and either way, something in him says *yes*. He can't explain it. He doesn't try. He just watches the water rise and feels something he hasn't felt in years: anticipation.
