# Waterborne Illness and Rural Medicine in PRL

## Choroby z brudnej wody

Contaminated well water in 1960s rural Poland caused a predictable set of illnesses:

- **Czerwonka bakteryjna (bacterial dysentery / shigellosis)** — the most common. Caused by *Shigella* bacteria. Severe bloody diarrhea, abdominal cramps, fever. Transmitted fecal-oral, typically through contaminated water. Children and the elderly most vulnerable.
- **Dur brzuszny (typhoid fever)** — *Salmonella Typhi*. Sustained fever, headache, abdominal pain. More dangerous than dysentery but less common from well water.
- **Salmonelloza** — broader *Salmonella* infections. Vomiting, diarrhea, fever.
- **Biegunki bakteryjne** — *E. coli*, *Campylobacter*, other gut bacteria. The "generic" waterborne diarrheal diseases.

### Why wells were dangerous

Village wells drew from shallow aquifers. Flood conditions raised the water table, mixing groundwater with surface contaminants — sewage, animal waste, decomposing organic matter. A well near a burial site (as in our scenario) would introduce phosphates, ammonia, and anaerobic bacteria from decomposition. The contamination wouldn't be visible — the water might look clear, smell faintly earthy, taste slightly mineral.

### Symptoms in a child (4 years old)

- **Day 1:** Watery diarrhea, low fever, irritability, loss of appetite
- **Day 2-3:** Fever spikes (39-40°C), bloody stool possible, severe dehydration — sunken eyes, dry mouth, skin loses elasticity, lethargy
- **Day 3-5 (without treatment):** Fever cycles — peaks and troughs. Each trough looks like recovery but isn't. Child becomes increasingly listless. Risk of seizures from dehydration and fever.
- **Day 5-7 (without antibiotics):** Most children survive bacterial dysentery through immune response alone, but recovery is slow and miserable. Mortality risk in a malnourished or isolated child: 5-10%.

### The dehydration clock

The real killer in childhood dysentery is dehydration, not the bacteria. A 4-year-old losing fluid through diarrhea and fever can reach critical dehydration in 24-48 hours. Modern ORT (oral rehydration therapy) packets didn't exist in the 1960s — treatment was improvised: boiled water with salt and sugar, rice water, broths. A competent person managing hydration could keep a child alive indefinitely, but the infection itself would cycle until either the immune system won or antibiotics intervened.

## Leczenie — sulfonamidy w PRL

### What sulfonamides are

Sulfonamides (sulfa drugs) were the first broadly effective antibacterials, predating penicillin. They work by blocking bacterial synthesis of folic acid — bacteria can't grow without it, but humans get folic acid from food, so the drug is selective. Discovered in the 1930s, they were the wonder drugs of WWII and remained the backbone of rural medicine in Poland well into the 1960s.

### Common sulfonamides in PRL

- **Norsulfazol (norsulfazolum)** — broad-spectrum, widely produced in Polish state pharmaceutical factories. The go-to sulfonamide for bacterial infections including dysentery. Cheap, effective, available everywhere.
- **Sulfaguanidyna** — specifically effective for gut infections (czerwonka, salmonelloza). Poorly absorbed from the gut, so it stays where the infection is.
- **Ftalylsulfatiazol** — for acute and chronic intestinal infections.
- **Sulfanilamid** — the original sulfa drug, still in use for simple infections.

### Dosing (historical)

For bacterial dysentery in a child: Norsulfazol 0.5g tablets, every 4-6 hours, for 5-7 days. First dose often doubled ("loading dose"). Treatment continued 2 days past symptom resolution. Side effects: nausea, skin rash, and — critically — kidney damage if the patient wasn't drinking enough water. A dehydrated child on sulfonamides needed careful fluid management.

### What sulfonamides can't do

- **Not effective against amoebic dysentery** (*Entamoeba histolytica*) — requires emetine or metronidazole
- **Not effective against viruses** — viral gastroenteritis looks similar but doesn't respond
- **Resistance was growing** — by the 1960s, overuse and improper dosing had created resistant bacterial strains. Tetracyclines and chloramphenicol were replacing sulfonamides in cities, but rural areas still relied on sulfa drugs because they were cheap and available.

### Recovery with medication

With proper sulfonamide treatment, bacterial dysentery in a child shows improvement within 12-24 hours. Fever breaks, diarrhea slows, appetite returns. Full recovery in 2-3 days. Without medication but with good hydration management, recovery takes 5-7 days and is much harder on the body.

## Dystrybucja leków na wsi — the pharmacy problem

### The PRL pharmaceutical chain

Medicine distribution in rural PRL was a hierarchy:

1. **Apteka (pharmacy)** — full range, licensed pharmacist. Found in towns, not villages.
2. **Punkt apteczny (pharmacy point)** — limited range, run by a pharmaceutical technician or trained nurse. Often attached to rural health centers (*wiejski ośrodek zdrowia*).
3. **Szafka apteczna (medicine cabinet)** — the smallest unit. A locked cabinet of basic medications kept by a designated person (teacher, sołtys, or store owner). Very limited stock: aspirin, bandages, iodine, basic sulfonamides, activated charcoal. Regulated by Rozporządzenie Ministra Zdrowia z 5 grudnia 1951 o punktach aptecznych i szafkach aptecznych.
4. **Sklep wiejski (village store)** — could sell only the most basic OTC items. Anything stronger required the szafka apteczna or punkt apteczny.

### In practice

In an isolated Bieszczady village in the 1960s, the most likely setup was a **szafka apteczna** kept in the village store, under the control of whoever owned or managed the store. The person controlling the cabinet controlled access to medicine. This was regulated — they needed basic training and had to keep records — but in practice, in remote villages, enforcement was loose and the person with the key had real power.

Sulfonamides like Norsulfazol were standard szafka apteczna stock — cheap, state-distributed, effective for the most common rural infections. They were available without a formal prescription in many cases, especially for known, common conditions like dysentery. But the cabinet was locked, and only the designated person could open it.

### The nearest real pharmacy

For a village in the Bieszczady near the Solina dam construction, the nearest full pharmacy (*apteka*) would be in **Lesko** or **Ustrzyki Dolne** — accessible by road in good weather, but after flooding? Roads washed out, bridges damaged. A child can't wait 2-3 days for roads to clear.

## Medycyna ludowa — Lemko and Bieszczady folk remedies

Traditional Lemko healing for gut infections used locally available plants:

### Piołun (Artemisia absinthium — wormwood)
- Strong antibacterial and antiparasitic properties
- Infusion of dried herb — one teaspoon per glass of hot water
- Extremely bitter; given in small sips
- Effective against bacterial and parasitic gut infections
- Used carefully — toxic in large doses, especially for children

### Kora dębu (Quercus — oak bark)
- High tannin content — powerful astringent and anti-inflammatory
- Decoction (boiled 15 minutes) drunk in small amounts
- "Ściąga jelita" — tightens the gut, reduces fluid loss
- The most commonly used folk remedy for diarrhea across all of rural Poland

### Krwawnik pospolity (Achillea millefolium — yarrow)
- Anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, mildly antibacterial
- Infusion drunk in small amounts, sometimes mixed with other herbs
- Regulates digestive function, reduces cramping

### Węgiel drzewny (charcoal)
- Activated charcoal from hardwood — adsorbs toxins in the gut
- Crushed and mixed with water
- Universal folk remedy for any kind of poisoning or gut trouble

### How folk medicine fits the timeline

A knowledgeable herbalist (like Paraskewia in our scenario) could prepare an effective treatment from forest plants within hours. The combination of wormwood (antibacterial), oak bark (astringent), and yarrow (anti-inflammatory) would genuinely slow the infection and reduce symptoms. It wouldn't work as fast as sulfonamides, but it would work. Recovery: 3-5 days instead of 1-2.

The Lemko tradition of combining herbal treatment with prayer and ritual wasn't just superstition — the calm environment, the structured routine, and the sense that someone competent was in charge all contributed to recovery. Stress hormones suppress immune function. A child surrounded by calm, purposeful activity recovers faster than one surrounded by panic.

## Campaign relevance

- **The medicine bottleneck is historically accurate.** In an isolated 1960s Bieszczady village after flooding, the szafka apteczna in the village store would genuinely be the only source of sulfonamide antibiotics. The person controlling it controls access to modern medicine.
- **Norsulfazol is the right drug.** Cheap, effective, standard PRL stock for exactly this kind of infection.
- **The folk medicine alternative is real.** Lemko herbalism could treat dysentery — slower, but functional.
- **The dehydration management scene is accurate.** A person with medical knowledge keeping a child hydrated with improvised ORT is doing exactly what field medicine would dictate.
- **Dr. Sawicki's phone advice is period-correct.** Rural doctors routinely diagnosed and prescribed over the phone when they couldn't reach patients. The specific questions (age, duration, stool colour) are the standard triage for childhood diarrheal disease.

## Dead bodies in water — myth vs reality

A common belief (and a narrative staple) is that corpses in water cause exotic plagues. **The WHO has debunked this.** Dead bodies from trauma — not infectious disease — are not a major source of epidemic pathogens. The bacteria and viruses that cause outbreaks (cholera, typhoid) don't survive long in a corpse, especially one that's been dead for years.

What decomposing bodies DO create:
- **Organic pollution** — cadaverine, putrescine (the smell of death), ammonia, phosphates
- **Bacterial breeding ground** — the organic load creates an environment where common waterborne bacteria (*E. coli*, *Shigella*, *Clostridium*) thrive
- **Chemical contamination** — ammonia and phosphates alter water chemistry in detectable ways
- **Taste and smell** — water tastes "mineral," "earthy," or simply wrong — even a child would notice

So a well containing dozens of bodies decomposing for 13 years (1947→1960) would NOT produce exotic corpse-plagues. It would produce **ordinary bacterial dysentery** — but from an extraordinary concentration of organic contamination. The disease is mundane. The source is mass murder.

### The medical detective angle

A trained medical professional examining the contamination pattern would notice:
- The bacterial load is too high for diffuse floodwater contamination
- The chemistry points to a **point source** — a confined space with concentrated organic decay
- The phosphate/ammonia signature indicates **large-scale** decomposition over **years**
- This isn't a flooded latrine or dead animal — the concentration suggests something much larger

This is how a Medicine skill check in the game becomes an investigation clue: the disease is common, but the *why* behind it points to something terrible.
