# Operation Vistula (Akcja Wisła) — 1947

## Overview

A forced resettlement operation carried out by the Polish communist government from April to July 1947. Approximately 140,000–150,000 ethnic Ukrainians, Lemkos, Boykos, and Rusyns were deported from southeastern Poland to the "Recovered Territories" (former German lands in western/northern Poland).

The stated justification was eliminating support for the Ukrainian Insurgent Army (UPA). Historians widely consider it ethnic cleansing designed to fragment and assimilate minorities.

## Timeline

### Prelude (1944–1947)

- **1944–1946:** ~480,000 Ukrainians transferred from Poland to the USSR in state-organized population exchanges.
- **28 March 1947:** General Karol Świerczewski assassinated near Baligród in the Bieszczady, allegedly by UPA. Used as pretext for the operation.
- **29 March 1947:** Polish Politburo resolves to forcibly relocate all Ukrainians and mixed families from the southeast.

### The Operation (April–July 1947)

- **17 April 1947:** State Committee on Public Security orders the operation.
- **24 April 1947:** Official decree by the Presidium of the Council of Ministers launches Akcja Wisła.
- **28 April 1947:** Military and security forces begin encircling Lemko, Boyko, and Ukrainian villages in Bieszczady and Beskid Niski.
- **28 April – 31 July 1947:** Villages surrounded at dawn. Residents given hours (sometimes as little as 30 minutes) to pack. Marched or transported to gathering points.
- **May–July 1947:** Deportees packed into freight cars — often with livestock, little food/water, dire sanitary conditions. Multi-day journeys to western/northern Poland. Deaths en route.
- **31 July 1947:** Official end of military phase. Sporadic deportations continue into 1950.
- **September 1947:** Final roundups of remaining individuals.

## Conditions

- Deportees could carry only what they could hold. Property, homes, farms seized.
- Families deliberately scattered — no community could be more than 10% from the same origin area. Designed to prevent cultural continuity.
- ~4,000 Ukrainians and Lemkos suspected of UPA ties were interned at **Jaworzno prison camp**.
- Return to the Bieszczady/Lemko region strictly forbidden until the 1950s.

## Bieszczady Villages Affected (Sample)

Baligród, Cisna, Polana, Wetlina, Ustrzyki Dolne, Wołosate, Brzegi Dolne, Polanki, Skorodne, Hulskie, Lutowiska, Smolnik, Zawój, Żubracze.

### Lemko region (Beskid Niski)

Komańcza, Zyndranowa, Olchowiec, Bartne, Ropianka, Losie.

## Aftermath

- Villages emptied overnight. Churches locked, icons left on walls. Cemeteries untended.
- Forests reclaimed farmland. Only old fruit trees and ruined foundations mark many sites.
- Cultural transmission shattered — language suppressed, children punished for speaking Lemko, clergy interned.
- Some returns began in the 1980s, but communities were permanently fragmented.
