# The Lemko People — Culture, Traditions & Daily Life

## Who Are the Lemkos?

An East Slavic ethnic group (Rusyn subgroup) indigenous to the Carpathian Mountains — primarily the **Beskid Niski** (Low Beskids) and parts of the Bieszczady in southeastern Poland. Settled by Vlachs and Ruthenians from the 14th–16th centuries, blending Eastern Slavic, Balkan, and local influences.

## Language

**Lemko** — a Rusyn dialect, East Slavic, influenced by Polish and Slovak. Distinct from Ukrainian though related. After deportation, children were punished for speaking it; transmission was severely disrupted.

## Religion

### Greek Catholic Church (majority)
- Byzantine rite but in communion with Rome (Uniate).
- Spiritual and social life revolved entirely around the parish church.
- Rich liturgical calendar — feasts, processions, saints' days structured the year.

### Eastern Orthodox (minority)
- Similar traditions, without Roman communion.

### Folk Beliefs
- Older pagan beliefs persisted alongside Christianity.
- Nature rituals, folk medicine, agricultural rites, superstitions around births/deaths/weather.
- "Rusalka Week" and other syncretic celebrations blending Christian and pre-Christian elements.

## Daily Life

### Economy
- Subsistence agriculture: rye, oats, potatoes, buckwheat.
- Pastoralism: sheep, goats, cattle. Sheep especially important (wool, cheese).
- Forestry, woodcarving, stonemasonry, weaving, beekeeping.
- Women managed households, gardens, crafts. Men worked fields, forests, took seasonal labor elsewhere.

### Social Structure
- Small, isolated villages along river valleys.
- Extended families often lived together.
- Strong mutual aid between neighbors — communal work was the norm.
- Tight-knit communities; everyone knew everyone.

### Food
- Simple, hearty, local. Staples: potatoes, dairy (especially **bryndza** — sheep's cheese), porridge, sauerkraut, beans, mushrooms.
- **Kapuśniak** — cabbage soup with smoked meat or mushrooms. Signature dish.
- **Fuczki** — sauerkraut pancakes.
- Home bread baking, cheese making.
- Foraging: wild berries, nuts, herbs.

### Clothing
- **Men:** Linen shirts, woolen trousers, wide leather belts, sheepskin vests/coats (*kożuch*), distinctive hats.
- **Women:** Embroidered blouses, long woolen skirts, aprons, kerchiefs, beaded necklaces.
- Festive clothes: intricate embroidery with floral/geometric motifs, bright colors.

### Customs & Celebrations
- Elaborate weddings with ancient ritual elements.
- Communal feasts for life events (births, deaths, harvests).
- Christmas caroling, Easter rites, seasonal celebrations.
- Pagan remnants in funeral practices and agricultural ceremonies.

## Architecture

### Homes (*chyża*)
- Wooden homesteads — long single buildings divided into living quarters and livestock area.
- Steeply pitched roofs, thatched or shingled.
- Simply furnished, hand-carved interior decoration.
- Designed for harsh mountain winters — family and animals under one roof for warmth.

### Churches (*cerkwie*)
- **Iconic wooden construction** — built without nails.
- Multi-domed onion roofs, richly decorated **iconostases** inside.
- Local conifers, shingle roofing (later some metal sheet covering).
- Some UNESCO-listed. Recognized as among the finest examples of Carpathian sacred architecture.
- After deportation: many left locked, fell into disrepair, or were repurposed. Some restored in modern era.

## The Destruction

After Operation Vistula (1947), Lemko villages were emptied. What remained:
- Churches with doors hanging open, icons fading on walls.
- Overgrown cemeteries with Cyrillic inscriptions.
- Fruit trees bearing in abandoned orchards — no one to pick them.
- Foundations being swallowed by forest.
- Personal belongings left behind in the rush to leave.

By the 1960s, when the Solina Dam project began, these ghost villages had stood empty for nearly 20 years. The flooding would erase the last physical traces.
