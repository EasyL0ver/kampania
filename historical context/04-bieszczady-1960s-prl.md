# Bieszczady in the 1960s — Life Under Communist Poland (PRL)

## Overview

After WWII and Operation Vistula, the Bieszczady region was one of the most depopulated, underdeveloped areas in Poland. Authorities called it the **"Polish Wild West"** — vast, empty, nearly lawless. The 1960s saw the state's first serious attempts to "civilize" it.

## Population & Settlement

- Most original inhabitants (Lemkos, Ukrainians, Boykos) deported in 1947.
- New settlers arrived from various backgrounds:
  - **Poles resettled from the eastern borderlands** (Kresy) lost to the USSR.
  - **"Bieszczady pioneers"** — people seeking freedom, adventure, or escape from city life.
  - Criminals and fugitives hiding from authorities.
  - State-directed settlers for forestry and infrastructure projects.
- Communities were a patchwork — strangers from different regions thrown together. No shared history, no established social fabric.

## Infrastructure & Roads

- **Severely lacking** well into the 1960s.
- Few paved roads. Most routes impassable in rain or mud.
- Construction of the **Wielka Pętla Bieszczadzka** (Great Bieszczady Loop) and **Mała Pętla** (Small Loop) road systems began in this decade — a flagship "civilization" project.
- **Military and prison labor** used for road construction.
- Before road improvements, some locations could only be reached via unmaintained paths or rail routes that passed through **Soviet territory**.
- Even after improvements, any journey could mean hours through rough, muddy terrain.

## Daily Life

- **Harsh and isolated.** Minimal access to utilities, healthcare, education.
- Subsistence farming, forestry work, or employment on state projects (timber industry, dam construction).
- Homes often in poor condition — repurposed from abandoned buildings or hastily constructed.
- Religious and cultural life muted. Many churches stood abandoned. Reminders of the pre-war multicultural population were everywhere — ruins, cemeteries, Cyrillic inscriptions — but officially unacknowledged.

## State Presence

- **Military and border guards (WOP)** were a major presence. Proximity to Soviet and Czechoslovak borders made the area strategically sensitive.
- Past UPA insurgency meant ongoing security paranoia.
- The state imposed socialist order through "social engineering" but the isolation and terrain limited political reach.
- Result: hybrid local communities emerged with their own informal rules and culture.

## Major State Projects in the 1960s

- **Solina Dam** (1960–1968) — largest infrastructure project. Brought workers, roads, and eventually tourism. Also displaced ~3,000 more people.
- Tourism development — trails, sanatoria, modest accommodation.
- Forestry expansion and light industry.

## Atmosphere — What It Felt Like

- Constant tension between the emptiness of the land and the state's ambitions for it.
- A landscape full of ghosts — abandoned villages, churches with no congregations, orchards with no farmers.
- Mud, rain, isolation. Days-long trips for basic supplies.
- A sense that the region was being used — its resources extracted, its history deliberately erased.
- The people who lived there were either displaced themselves or had chosen to disappear into the wilderness.
