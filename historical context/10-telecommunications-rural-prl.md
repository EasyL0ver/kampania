# Telecommunications in Rural PRL — 1960s

## Overview

Telephone access in rural Poland during the 1960s was virtually nonexistent. The Bieszczady region — already the most isolated and underdeveloped part of the country — was even worse off than the average village.

## Key Facts

- **One phone per village** was typical — located at the post office, gmina office, or sołtys's house. Not in private homes.
- **Manual switchboard operators** connected calls. You picked up, asked the operator for a number, and waited. Quality was terrible, connections dropped, bad weather could knock lines out entirely.
- **Waiting lists** for a private phone were **10–15 years** in cities. On a Bieszczady village? Forget it.
- **Common models:** RWT CB-621, CB-662, AWS-1 — heavy bakelite handsets, rotary dial or crank-operated.
- **Priority access** went to state institutions, party officials, PGR state farms, and the military. A sołtys with a phone in his office was a man with real power — he controlled the village's only link to the outside world.

## What This Means for the Story

- The committee cannot easily contact the outside. The only phone is in %WOJEWODA%'s office — and he decides who uses it.
