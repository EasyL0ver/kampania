# Wolves in 1960s Bieszczady

Research on wolf presence in the Bieszczady mountains during the PRL era, relevant to the scenario's wolf subplot.

## Population and Habitat

After WWII, Akcja Wisła emptied hundreds of Lemko and Boyko villages across the Bieszczady. Forests reclaimed settlements within a decade. The depopulation created ideal wolf habitat — vast tracts of uninhabited mountain forest with growing wild prey populations (deer, boar) and decreasing human presence.

By the 1960s wolves were present in the Bieszczady but in **low numbers.** PRL policy treated wolves as pests — systematic culling campaigns, bounties on kills, poison bait stations. The goal was protecting state agricultural livestock. As a result, packs were small and fragmented, typically 4–7 animals.

## Relationship to Settlers

The new colonists who settled the emptied Bieszczady in the 1950s had little experience with wolves. Most came from lowland regions where wolves had been exterminated generations earlier. They inherited a landscape where:

- Wolves were **real but rarely seen** — shy, nocturnal, avoiding humans
- Livestock was the primary point of conflict — sheep, goats, young cattle, stray dogs
- **Attacks on humans were essentially nonexistent** — no documented cases of healthy wild wolves attacking people in postwar Poland
- Fear far exceeded reality — settlers told stories of bloodthirsty packs, inherited from folk memory and amplified by isolation

## Livestock Predation

Wolf attacks on PGR livestock were a documented problem in remote Bieszczady:

- Sheep were the primary target — easiest prey, poorest fencing
- Attacks typically occurred at night, especially during periods of bad weather when wild prey scattered
- A single wolf could kill multiple sheep in one attack (surplus killing — instinctive, not hunger-driven)
- PGR farms in remote areas lacked resources for proper predator-proofing: basic wooden fences, no guardian dogs bred for the purpose, no night shepherds
- The state response was culling and bounties, not livestock protection

## Behaviour Patterns

- Wolves avoid human settlements and retreat from human presence
- They are most active at dusk and dawn, with significant nighttime activity
- Howling serves territorial communication — a pack's howl can carry 10+ km through mountain valleys
- Heavy rain and flooding displaces prey and pushes wolves to lower elevations
- Wolves are neophobic (fear new things) — but habituate to consistent stimuli over time. A person living in their territory for years would eventually be treated as part of the landscape, not a threat.

## Cultural Context — Slavic Wolf Folklore

In Carpathian and Lemko folk tradition, wolves carry deep symbolic weight:

- **Psychopomps** — associated with Weles (Veles), patron of the dead and the underworld. Wolves are boundary-guardians who patrol the threshold between the living and the dead. They guide souls between worlds.
- **Taboo naming** — people avoided saying "wilk" (wolf) aloud to prevent summoning one. Euphemisms like "szary" (the grey one) were used instead.
- **Wilkołak (werewolf)** — transformation into a wolf as curse, punishment, or penance. In Carpathian tradition, the wilkołak often carried moral weight — a soul forced into wolf-form for transgression.
- **Protectors and devourers** — paradoxically, wolves were both feared and respected. They could devour evil spirits. Meeting a wolf was sometimes considered a good omen. A wolf crossing your path could be a warning or a blessing.
- **Liminal creatures** — wolves inhabit the boundary between civilisation and wilderness, human and animal, control and instinct. The same symbolic space as the well.

## Scenario Relevance

The wolf subplot sits at the intersection of documented reality and folk belief. Wolves attacking PGR livestock during heavy rains is historically plausible — even expected. Wolves circling an abandoned village and howling at night is normal territorial behaviour. A solitary forest-dweller coexisting with a local pack is biologically unremarkable.

But for villagers steeped in Carpathian folk memory — even villagers who've never been to the Carpathians — the wolves carry older meanings. The grey ones at the threshold. The dead restless. The forest closing in.
